## Relevant artefacts

### Four sophisticated-attacker characteristics (Persistent / Adaptive / Stealthy / Incentive-driven)

**Source locator:** §V-A *Characteristics of Advanced Advances* (source lines 375–400; no PDF page numbers in markdown extract).

**Paraphrase:** Cho et al. enumerate four characteristics observed across the MTD literature's treatment of sophisticated adversaries. *Persistent*: attackers operate across multiple stages (reconnaissance through exploitation), not as one-off intrusions, aligned with APT-style campaigns. *Adaptive*: attackers respond to dynamically changing system and environmental conditions, including resource awareness, and pursue opportunistic compromise. *Stealthy*: attackers do not exhibit identifiable attack behaviour continuously; they blend in until the moment that inflicts the most harm. *Incentive-driven*: attackers are intelligent enough to pursue cost-effective outcomes (maximal effect for minimal cost) — i.e. modelled as rational actors sensitive to incentive signals. The section explicitly links these to APT operations and the Cyber Kill Chain framing that opens §V-B.

**Quote (essential — the rational-actor framing on the attacker side):**
> "we can consider the attacker to be a rational actor that is sensitive to incentives, such as attack success with minimum cost" (§V-A, *Incentive-driven attackers*).

**Maps to:** [`../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md`](../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md) §IV-B (Table II axis #1: Persistent / Adaptive / Stealthy / Incentive-aware). Supports [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(a) attacker-side framing and the §(f) Jalowski-primitives encoding decision.

**Disposition for this thesis:** *adopted-as-baseline* — used as the qualitative axis against which the §IV-B cross-section's threat models are scored. The thesis itself does not advance new attacker characteristics; it instead pairs Cho's characteristics with a *fidelity descriptor* (constructed in [`../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md`](../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md) §IV-B) that distinguishes parametric / scripted / procedural / behavioural realisation depth — i.e. how *deeply* a given evaluation operationalises any one characteristic, not which characteristics it names.

### Three under-developed dimensions of the attacker model

**Source locator:** §V-D *Limitations of the Current Attack Models* (source lines 454–464).

**Paraphrase:** Cho et al. close §V with three structural under-developments in how MTD literature models attackers, drawn from their preceding survey of attack types. (1) *Smart, intelligent attackers are significantly less considered* — defenders are routinely granted ML or cognitive-learning capability while attackers are assumed to follow fixed patterns, an asymmetry Cho et al. explicitly observe runs contrary to practice. (2) *Few scenarios consider multiple strategies by attackers and defenders* — most work pits one MTD mechanism against one attack path, leaving the multi-strategy regime (attacker chooses across exploits, defender chooses across mechanisms) under-treated. (3) *An attacker has been less considered as a rational decision maker with learning ability* — the rational-actor framing is applied asymmetrically: routinely on the defender side, seldom on the attacker side.

**Quote (essential — the asymmetry observation, used directly in [`../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md`](../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md) §IV-A):**
> "Generally, it is assumed that attackers have certain attack patterns, rather than that they learn and can launch adaptive attacks. Whereas the contrary is true for defenders; they are often permitted to take proactive, adaptive defense actions based on an assumed highly intelligent learning capability. This assumption may not be true; even the attackers are often smarter than the defenders in practice" (§V-D, *Smart, intelligent attackers*).

**Maps to:** [`../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md`](../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md) §IV-A lines 151–157 (the surveys-naming-the-gap paragraph); [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(a) framing and §(j) methodological positioning (the dominant pattern the thesis is positioned *against*).

**Disposition for this thesis:** *adopted-as-baseline* — these three dimensions are the diagnostic the thesis takes forward, paired six years later with [[jalowski2026]]'s restatement of the same gap. The thesis does not claim to close all three — its scoped contribution is sensitivity-analysis around the rational-actor and learning-capability asymmetries via CTI-grounded behavioural profiles ([`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(a), §(k)). The multi-strategy dimension is partly addressed through varying the defence pool across SDR families × motivation profiles, but the *attacker* side of multi-strategy (branching across exploits adaptively) sits within the pending Jalowski primitives ([`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(f), Decision-block).

### SDR operation-based MTD classification (shuffling / diversity / redundancy)

**Source locator:** §III-B *Operation-Based MTD* (source lines 237–268); SDR-by-technique detail in §IV-A–C (lines 282–338).

**Paraphrase:** Cho et al. adopt the operation-based classification originally proposed in Hong and Kim [76] (cited as [[hong2018]] in this corpus), structuring MTD techniques by the operational nature of the change rather than by what is being changed or when. *Shuffling* rearranges or randomises system configurations (IP mutation, port hopping, VM migration, topology shuffle, OS rotation) to invalidate attacker reconnaissance or waste attacker resources. *Diversity* deploys functionally-equivalent components with different implementations (software-stack diversity, code diversity, programming-language diversity, network diversity) so that an exploit against one variant is unlikely to transfer to others. *Redundancy* replicates components to preserve service availability under compromise (redundant software components, redundant network sessions). The three are complementary rather than mutually exclusive: §III-B explicitly notes shuffling can enhance diversity, and high diversity + effective shuffling support redundancy.

**Maps to:** [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(h) Glossary `MTD mechanism family — SDR` (which already cross-links this extraction); [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(i) substrate seam (MTD mechanism pool); [`../specs/mtdsim_spec.md`](../specs/mtdsim_spec.md) MTD-01–MTD-09 (the SDR-family rows in the substrate spec).

**Disposition for this thesis:** *adopted-as-baseline* — SDR is the classification under which the thesis's frozen defence pool is enumerated. The defender side is held constant ([`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(a) Decision-block: *defence side is existing mechanisms only*), so SDR is inherited as the partition that names the MTD-family axis of the L4 evaluation matrix.

### Four-method validation classification (analytical / simulation / emulation / testbed)

**Source locator:** §VIII *Evaluation Methods for MTD* (source lines 679–731). The analytical category is further split in §VIII-A into (1) probabilistic and (2) graphical-security-model approaches (lines 683–704).

**Paraphrase:** Cho et al. classify MTD evaluation methods into four categories, distinguished by the relationship between the evaluation artefact and the real system. *Analytical models* describe attacker / defender / system behaviour by mathematical parameterisation, split between probabilistic models (e.g., Stochastic Petri Nets, attack-success-probability models) and graphical security models (attack graphs, attack trees, HARM). *Simulation models* parameterise design features in a software substrate that executes scenarios numerically. *Emulation models* run real-system components in a controlled environment (e.g., Mininet) to gain validity at the cost of scale. *Real testbeds* implement MTD on production-like infrastructure (SDN testbeds, cloud platforms) for the highest validity but lowest scale. The four are characterised by a tradeoff between flexibility and validity; pros/cons are tabulated in Cho's Table VI (§VIII, line 717).

**Maps to:** [`../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md`](../sources/LIT_REVIEW.md) §III prose (line 59): *"Cho et al. [2] classify these into four categories — analytical, simulation, emulation, and real testbed — with the analytical category splitting into probabilistic and graphical-security-model approaches."* Supports [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(i) Decision-block (*the inherited HARM-structural + DES-execution composition is treated as a deliberate choice, not a default*) — the four methods are *components* that the GSM-MTD literature composes, not mutually-exclusive alternatives.

**Disposition for this thesis:** *adopted-as-baseline* — the thesis inherits the *simulation + graphical-security-model* composition (HARM structural representation, DES execution via MTDSim) from the lineage. The other three categories (probabilistic analytical, emulation, real testbed) are scoped out per [`../specs/architecture.md`](../specs/architecture.md) §(a) (no MITRE Caldera emulation) and §(f) (Petri-net analytical positioned as parallel candidate, not adopted here). The composition is treated as a deliberate inheritance, not a default — see the §(i) Decision-block.
